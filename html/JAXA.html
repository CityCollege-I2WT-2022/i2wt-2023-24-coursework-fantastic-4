Brief Overview

The Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency (JAXA) is the Japanese national air and space agency.JAXA, established on October 1, 2003, undertakes the development of technology and research, as well as launching satellites into space. It also participates in sophisticated endeavors, such as investigating asteroids and exploring the potential for manned missions to the Moon.JAXA has an important role in international collaborations, pushing the boundaries of what is achievable, leading satellite technology to the exploration of new renewable energy sources extending even to space solar power concepts.

History

On 1 October 2003, three organizations were merged to form the new JAXA: Japan's Institute of Space and Astronautical Science (ISAS), the National Aerospace Laboratory of Japan (NAL), and National Space Development Agency of Japan (NASDA). JAXA was formed as an Independent Administrative Institution administered by the Ministry of Education, Culture, Sports, Science and Technology (MEXT) and the Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications (MIC). ISAS was responsible for space and planetary research, NAL was responsible on aviation research and NASDA, which had been founded in 1969 had developed rockets, satellites and the Japanese Experiment Module. Japan was already a historic figure in space exploration and research. Japan focused on developing a launch vehicle that could carry small satellites into orbit. The first Japanese satellite, Ohsumi, was launched in 1970.In the 1970s, Japan began to develop more ambitious space missions. Noteworthy was the 1985 launch of Sakigake, Japan's first interplanetary spacecraft, which made a flyby of Halley's Comet in 1986. Moreover, the H-II rocket, which would become a cornerstone of Japan’s space capability, was under development during this decade.Japan's space endeavors in the 1990s included more ambitious projects. The Advanced Satellite for Cosmology and Astrophysics (ASCA, originally named ASTRO-D) was launched in 1993 and was significant for X-ray astronomy. NASDA continued to improve its launch vehicle technology with the H-II rocket successfully launching in 1994. By the end of the decade, Japan commenced its involvement with the International Space Station (ISS) program.

Notable JAXA Missions

  • H-IIA Launch Vehicle – An active series of launch vehicles used for a variety of payloads and purposes since its first launch in 2001.
  • Hayabusa (MUSES-C) – Launched in 2003, this spacecraft successfully returned samples from the asteroid Itokawa in 2010.
  • SELENE (Kaguya) – This lunar orbiter mission launched in 2007 to study the moon's origin and evolution.
  • Akatsuki (PLANET-C) – Launched in 2010, and after a failed attempt at Venus orbit insertion, it succeeded in 2015.
  • IKAROS (Interplanetary Kite-craft Accelerated by Radiation Of the Sun) – Launched in 2010, it's the first spacecraft to successfully demonstrate solar sail technology in interplanetary space.
  • Hayabusa2 – Launched in 2014 to collect samples from the asteroid Ryugu, with a scheduled return to Earth in December 2020.
  • ASTRO-H (Hitomi) – Launched in 2016, an X-ray astronomy satellite.
  • GCOM-C1 (Shikisai) – Launched in 2017, designed for climate observation.
  • GPM (Global Precipitation Measurement) Core Observatory – A joint mission with NASA launched in 2014 to measure global precipitation.
  • QZSS (Quasi-Zenith Satellite System) – An ongoing regional satellite navigation system designed to enhance GPS in the Asia-Oceania regions with several satellites launched after 2010.
  • Kounotori (H-II Transfer Vehicle, HTV) – A series of missions beginning in 2009 providing cargo resupply services to the International Space Station.
  • BepiColombo – Joint mission with ESA, launched in 2018, on its way to Mercury.
