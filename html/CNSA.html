Chinese Space Program 中国国家航天局

Brief Overview

The China National Space Administration (CNSA) is the government space agency of the republic of China with each headquarters located in Haidian, Beijing. The CNSA is responsible for space programs administration and international cooperation with other agencies. The CNSA was founded in 1993 and is an administrative agency under the Ministry of Industry and Information Technology. Although The China National Space Administration does not carry out any space missions but rather serves as the administrative authority for civil space endeavors. The task of implementing China’s national space initiatives falls to the China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation. When it comes to the China Manned Space Program, it is specifically managed by the China Manned Space Agency, not the CNSA.

Birth and Development Through the 1950s and 1960s

China is historically one of the most active countries in space explorations and research around space oriented subjects . China's evolvement in space exploration and research begins in the 1950s. The year of it's birth was 1949 and from the early 1950s missile research was undergoing mainly focused on the military needs of the republic in the era of the cold war. In 1955, the renowned rocket scientist Qian Xuesen came back to China from the United States. The following year, he presented a plan for creating China's missile technology, which received authorization after only a few months. On October 8, China established its missile research institute, known as the Fifth Research Academy, under the the Ministry of National Defense, staffing it with under 200 employees, many of whom were personally enlisted by Qian. This milestone is celebrated as the origin of China's space endeavors. China cooperating secretly with the Soviet union was able to start manufacturing a licensed copy of two Soviet R-2 missiles, which were secretly shipped to China in December 1957 as part of the cooperative technology transfer program between the Soviet Union and China . The Chinese version of the missile was named 1059 and was intended to launch in 1959 but the split of relationship and cooperated with USSR stopped and the program got a major delay. Meanwhile, China started constructing its first missile test site in the Gobi desert of Inner Mongolia, which later became the famous Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center, China's first spaceport. The space race pushed China to want to be in par with the superpowers of the era. Despite lacking techical knowledge the Chinese endeavors were started to be more realistic. The missile 1059 was later renamed as Dongfeng-1 and was ultimately launch on December 1960. Dongfend-2 was already under manufacturing and was intended to be the first missile completely build by China .DF-2 achieved its first successful launch on its second attempt on Jun 29, 1964 in Jiuquan. It was considered as a major milestone in China's missile development history. Almost all of China's new aerospace work units in the late 1960s and early 1970s were established as part of the Third Front and Third Front projects included expansion of Jiuquan Satellite Launch Center, building Xichang Satellite Launch Center, and building Taiyuan Satellite Launch Center.

Cruising Through 1970/80/90s

After Mao Zedong's death in 1976, the political balance in China changed and many changes started happening.Despite initial development slowdowns and the cancellation of various defense projects like the Fanji ABM and land-based anti-missile laser programs, advancements continued in other areas. China commissioned its first Yuanwang-class space tracking ship in 1979 and successfully tested the DF-5 ICBM in 1980, striking a target 9300 km away. Economic development in the late 1970s, made China realize that there was a need for communication satellites and that leaded to Project 331 starting in 1975. This resulted in the Dong Fang Hong 2 (DFH-2) communication satellites, developed under Sun Jiadong. The Xichang Satellite Launch Center was selected for its ideal latitude for GTO launches.The satellite attained its designated geostationary orbit by April 16 and was officially operational for the end user by May 14. This achievement marked China's debut in operating its own geostationary communication satellite, establishing it as the fifth nation globally to independently develop and launch satellites into geostationary orbit. Following this milestone, on February 1, 1986, China launched its first operational DFH-2 communications satellite using the Long March 3 rocket, marking the end of its dependency on foreign communications satellites. In 1986, China initiated Project 863 to propel advanced technological development, including human spaceflight. This brought advancements of the Long March rockets, such as the successful Long March 3, which allowed China to enter the commercial space launch market. China had it's first international commercial launch contract with AsiaSat in 1989 to launch the AsiaSat 1 communications satellite. On April 7, 1990, the Long March 3 rocket succeeded in delivering AsiaSat 1 into geosynchronous transfer orbit. Further developments brought Long March 3B which eventually became a cornerstone of China's launch capabilities consistently delivering heavy satellites to orbit. Organization changes in the 1990s led to the creation of the China Aerospace Science and Technology Corporation (CASC) and China Aerospace Science and Industry Corporation (CASIC) in 1999.

Notable CNSA Missions

    Shenzhou Program:

  • Shenzhou 1 (1999, Nov): Test flight.
  • Shenzhou 2 (2001, Jan): Carried animals as an experimental payload.
  • Shenzhou 3 (2002, Mar): Carried a test dummy and science experiments.
  • Shenzhou 4 (2002, Dec): Carried a human dummy and science experiments, preparing for crewed missions.
  • Shenzhou 5 (2003, Oct): First crewed spaceflight by China, with Yang Liwei becoming the first Chinese astronaut in space.
  • Shenzhou 6 (2005, Oct): Second crewed mission, with two astronauts.
  • Shenzhou 7 (2008, Sep): Featured China’s first spacewalk.
  • Shenzhou 8 (2011, Nov): Uncrewed mission, part of China's space docking tests.
  • Shenzhou 9 (2012, Jun): Crewed mission including China's first female astronaut, Liu Yang.
  • Shenzhou 10 (2013, Jun): Crewed mission, with a total of 3 astronauts.
  • Shenzhou 11 (2016, Oct): Crewed mission docking with Tiangong-2 space laboratory.

Lunar Exploration—Chang'e Program:

  • Chang'e 1 (2007, Oct): Lunar orbiting spacecraft.
  • Chang'e 2 (2010, Oct): Lunar orbiter, then an asteroid probe
  • Chang'e 3 (2013, Dec): Soft-landed the Yutu rover on the Moon.
  • Chang'e 4 (2019, Jan): First mission to land on the far side of the Moon with the Yutu-2 rover.
  • Chang'e 5 (2020, Nov): Lunar sample-return mission.

Mars Exploration:

  • Tianwen-1 (2020, Jul): Consists of an orbiter, lander, and the Zhurong rover for Mars exploration.

Tiangong Space Station:

  • Tiangong-1 (2011, Sep): China's first prototype space station module.
  • Tiangong-2 (2016, Sep): Space lab to prepare for the construction of a large space station.
