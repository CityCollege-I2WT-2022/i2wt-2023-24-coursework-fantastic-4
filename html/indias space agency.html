Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO)

Brief Overview

Isro is the national space agency of India . Isro is the main operator of the research of the India Department of Space (Dos) and is responsible for space oriented missions and subjects as space exploration, international space cooperation and the development of related technologies . Isro is one of the most advanced national space programs and one of the 6 that has full launch capabilitiescan deploy cryogenic engines, can launch extraterrestrial missions and operate a large fleet of artificial satellites. And also is one of the 4 government space agencies that have soft landing capabilities

Foundations of Indias Space Endeavors

"India is one of the earliest nations to engage in space-related research. In the 1920s, pioneering experiments began, with S.K. Mitra conducting ground-based radio soundings of the ionosphere. Additionally, two other scientists, C.V. Raman and Meghnad Saha, played a crucial role in developing fundamental scientific principles applicable to space sciences. However, the first significant advancements occurred after 1945, originated by two scientists: Vikram Sarabhai, the founder of the Physical Research Laboratory in Ahmedabad, and Homi Bhabha, who established the Tata Institute of Fundamental Research in 1945. These pioneering efforts encompassed a wide range of experiments, including cosmic radiation studies, high-altitude and airborne testing, in-depth underground experimentation at the Kolar gold mines one of the world's deepest mining sites and investigations of the upper atmosphere. During the 1950s, the Department of Atomic Energy was established and assumed responsibility for funding the space program. In 1954, the Aryabhatta Research Institute of Observational Sciences (ARIES) came into being. In 1962, the Indian National Committee for Space Research (INCOSPAR) was established under Prime Minister Jawaharlal Nehru. From 1967, India possessed an indigenous series of sounding rockets dubbed Rohini, which underwent further development and launched successfully.

ISRO Through the 1970s, 1980s and 1990s

In the 1970s under the government of Indira Gandhi ISRO had already superseded INCOSPAR, initializing space research in India and forming the Indian space program into the form it had today. India participated in the Soviet Interkosmos program and got its first Satellite Aryabhatatta in orbit through a Soviet Rocket. In 1979 ISRO launched the SLV launch Pads first launch but the satellite into its desired orbit. It was followed by a successful launch in 1980 carrying a Rohini Series-I satellite. India was the seventh country to reach earths orbit after the USSR, the US, UK, France, China and Japan. RS-1 was the third Indian satellite to reach orbit as Bhaskara had been launched from the USSR in 1979. SRO's Liquid Propulsion Systems Centre (LPSC) was set up in 1985 and started working on a more powerful engine, Vikas and two years later, facilities to test liquid-fuelled rocket engines were established and development and testing of various rocket engines thrusters began. Further research has lead to the Development of PSLV(polar satellite launch vehicle) that boosted the India’s space program as the 1990s arrived. PSLV became the main launch vehicle with over 50 successful flights. India was able to launch low Earth orbit satellites, small payloads to GTO and hundreds of foreign satellites.

21st Century and the Future of ISRO

In the 2000s Indias Prime Minister Atal Bihari Vajpayee urged scientists to develop technologies to land humans on the Moon. ISRO focused its resources on lunar programs, planetary and crewed missions. In 2008 Chandrayaan-1 was launched, which was the first Indian lunar probe under the Chandrayaan programme and operated until August 2009. Its mission aimed to comprehensively map the chemical composition and topography of the lunar surface over an extended two-year period. The polar regions held particular significance as they were suspected of harboring water ice deposits. Among its notable accomplishments was the detection of widespread water molecules embedded within lunar soil. In 2013 in Mars Orbiter Mission , known as Mangalyaan, which succeded to enter Martian orbit in its first try making India the first country to achieve this. In 23 August 2023, India achieved the very first soft landing on an extraterrestrial body and became the first nation to successfully land a spacecraft near the lunar south pole with ISRO's Chandrayaan-3, the third Moon mission. On September 2 of 2023 India successfully launched its first sun probe the Aditya-L1
